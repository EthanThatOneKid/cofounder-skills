# Cofounder Launch Skill

This skill guides you through the **Sell** phase of the Cofounder Tech Tree — building a brand presence, getting your first users, and running your initial sales motion.

## When to Use

- User has a live product and needs to get users
- User says "how do I get my first customers?"
- User needs to build a website or marketing presence
- User is defining their ICP or sales motion
- User wants help with outreach or marketing

---

## Phase 1: Build the Website

Your website is the home base for distribution. Sales emails link to it. Customers use it to understand what you do. Marketing campaigns send people there.

**The first thing your website should do is tell any reader what your company does.**

### A Good Early Website Answers

- Who is this for?
- What painful problem does it solve?
- What does the product do?
- Why is this better than the current way?
- What should the visitor do next?

For a live product: **Sign up**, **Book a demo**, or **Log in**.
For an earlier product: **Join the waitlist** or **Talk to us**.

### Keep It Simple

You need:
- A clear homepage
- A simple product explanation
- A few screenshots or visuals
- One call to action

Do not overcomplicate the first version. Ship it, then iterate.

### Website Structure Recommendation

```
/ (homepage)
├── Hero — what it is, who it's for, CTA
├── Problem — the pain you're solving
├── Product — how it works
├── Social proof — early users, testimonials
├── Pricing (if self-serve)
└── CTA — sign up / contact
```

## Phase 2: Get Your First User

Start with people close enough to give real feedback — friends, former coworkers, industry contacts, advisors.

**Do not just send a link and ask "what do you think?"** Watch them use the product if you can. Ask them to share their screen. Sit next to them. Pay attention to where they hesitate, what they misunderstand, and what they try to do that isn't supported yet.

### Questions to Answer in User Research

1. **Do they understand what the product is for?** Watch for confusion in the first 60 seconds.
2. **Can they reach the first valuable moment?** Count the steps. Every extra step is a drop-off risk.
3. **Where do they get confused?** Hesitation, misclicks, misread labels = data.
4. **What would make them use it again?** Retention starts on day one.
5. **Would they pay for it?** Willingness to pay is the strongest signal.
6. **Who else has this problem?** The start of your lead list and ICP.

Take feedback seriously, but do not obey every request. Users are good at revealing problems. They are not always good at designing solutions.

## Phase 3: Define Your ICP

Most people start too broad. "We sell to founders" is too vague for sales.

You need an **Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)** — the highly specific customer you are targeting first.

**Too broad:** "We sell to ecommerce companies."
**Specific:** "We sell to DTC brands doing $5M–$50M in revenue that rely on repeat purchases and have a small retention team."

The specific version tells you: where to look, who to message, what pain to lead with, what language to use.

### ICP Dimensions

For each dimension, go from broad to specific:

| Dimension | Broad | Specific |
|---|---|---|
| Company size | Businesses | DTC brands, $5M–$50M revenue |
| Industry | Retail | Beauty, apparel, supplements |
| Growth stage | Growing | Recently raised Series A |
| Geography | US | US + Canada |
| Workflow | Marketing | Retention marketing |
| Budget | Has budget | $5K–$20K/year on current tools |
| Trigger event | — | Just hired a head of retention |

A **trigger event** is something that makes the problem urgent: a company just raised money, a new leader joined, a team is hiring, a regulation changed.

## Phase 4: Understand Your Competitors

Competitors are proof that customers already care about the problem. The mistake is copying them.

Search the main keyword in your category and study the top companies.

**Look at:**
- Who their homepage speaks to
- What pain they lead with
- Which features they highlight
- How they price
- What their reviews complain about

**Then ask:** *What are they missing?*

Maybe they serve enterprises and ignore smaller teams. Maybe they're powerful but hard to use. Maybe customers like the outcome but hate the workflow. That gap is where you position yourself.

## Phase 5: Choose Your Sales Motion

A sales motion is how you sell. The right motion depends on:

1. **Transactional or consultative?** — Can the buyer decide quickly without talking to you?
2. **Product-led or relationship-led?** — Does the product experience sell itself, or does trust matter more?
3. **Inbound or outbound?** — Do customers come to you, or do you go to them?

**Simple rules:**
- Cheap, obvious, low-risk → self-serve and product-led
- Expensive, complex, high-trust → consultative sales
- Nobody knows you exist yet → you need outbound

Choose the simplest motion that can close your first customers.

### Sales Motion Matrix

| | Buyers Are Searching | You Need Outbound |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple / Low Risk** | Product-Led Inbound | Low-Touch Outbound |
| **Complex / High Trust** | Sales-Assisted Inbound | Founder-Led Consultative |

Most startups start in **outbound** because nobody knows they exist yet.

## Phase 6: Set Up the Sales Workflow

A simple early pipeline:

```
Open → Trying to Contact → Contacted → Consult → Pitch → 
Verbal Commit → Closed Won (or Closed Lost)
```

Deal should only move when something objective has happened.

### Run a Consult Call

The best consult calls feel like real conversations. You are listening for:
- What problem are they trying to solve?
- Why does it matter now?
- How do they handle it today?
- What is broken about the current approach?
- What happens if they do nothing?
- Who else is involved in the decision?
- What would a successful outcome look like?

### Develop a Strong Pitch

A pitch is not a deck. It's the story you tell about the customer, their problem, and the better outcome you can help create.

**Structure:**
1. **Here is what we heard** — repeat the customer's problem in their language
2. **Here is why it matters** — connect pain to time, money, risk, growth
3. **Here is what changes** — show the better workflow
4. **Here is how the product helps** — explain only as much as needed
5. **Here is proof** — examples, testimonials, data, demo
6. **Here is the next step** — make the path forward obvious

### Close the Deal

A good close makes buying feel safe. Make sure you have:
- Clear price
- Clear scope
- Clear buyer
- Clear timeline
- Clear next meeting or signature date

**Not a close:** "Let me know what you think."
**A close:** "Can we send the order form today and schedule onboarding for next Tuesday?"

## Phase 7: Run Outreach

Cold outreach works best when the prospect can also find a real website, a real point of view, and real signs that your company understands the market.

**Good cold email:**
- References a trigger event
- Names a specific problem
- Explains what the product does
- Asks for a small next step

**Example:**
> [Trigger], which means [problem]. We help [ICP] [outcome] without [current pain]. Worth a quick look next week?

## Phase 8: Do Marketing

Marketing creates awareness and generates warm leads. Sales converts those leads into customers.

### Content Types by Customer

- **Developers** → technical guides, API docs, code examples
- **Operators** → templates, workflows, case studies
- **Executives** → industry reports, ROI calculators, whitepapers

### Tell a Consistent Story

Pick 2–3 channels that fit your ICP and do them consistently. Do not try to be everywhere at once.

A consistent story means: the same brand voice, the same core message, the same visual identity — whether it's a tweet, a blog post, or a sales email.
